Master's Thesis: Understanding Metallic Nanoparticle Synthesis

My Master's thesis investigated the Pulsed Laser Ablation (PLAL) method for synthesizing metallic nanoparticles, with a focus on Aluminum Nanoparticles (ANPs).

Read the full thesis paper on ResearchGate

Initial simulation setup

Nanoparticle formation over time

Structural analysis of ANPs

Effect of temperature decrease rate

Final state of synthesized nanoparticles

Here's what I explored and achieved:

  • Simulated Nanoparticle Formation: Conducted Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations using the ReaxFF reactive force field in LAMMPS to analyze the phase transition and behavior of aluminum (Al) nanoparticles.
  • Investigated Temperature Dependence: Examined the impact of varying temperature decrease rates (0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, and 10.0 K/ps) on ANP formation from an initial plasma state of 5000 K down to 300 K.
  • Analyzed Nanoparticle Characteristics:
    • Quantified nanoparticle formation rates and growth of the biggest nanoparticles.
    • Performed neighbor analysis to understand particle attachment and stability within clusters.
    • Determined the size and structural properties of ANPs using cluster analysis and Polyhedral Template Matching (PTM).
    • Described physical properties (volume, density, energy) to validate simulation stability.
  • Compared Simulation Outcomes: Provided a comparative analysis of nanoparticle count, size (by particle number, mesh surface, and diameter), and size distribution across different cooling rates.
  • Key Finding: Demonstrated that the rate of nanoparticle formation and their ultimate size are directly influenced by the pace of temperature drop, suggesting a powerful mechanism for controlling PLAL synthesis efficiency.
  • Bridged Experimental Gaps: Highlighted how MD simulations can overcome limitations in traditional experimental analysis of metallic behavior at high temperatures and in plasma states.
